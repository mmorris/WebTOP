Fresnel Number

It follows directly from Eq. (1) that for on-axis observation points, the intensity is zero if the Fresnel number is even and equal to four times the incident intensity if the Fresnel number is odd. Also, starting at one side of the intensity pattern and proceeding across the other side, the number of major maxima seen is equal to the Fresnel number.

When the Fresnel number is much less than 1, say 0.1 or less, the observation point in question is said to be in the Fraunhofer region. When the Fresnel number is greater than or equal to 1, the observation point in question is said to be in the Fresnel region.

Eq. (8) is one of the most famous results in all of optics. It tells us that there is a bright spot on-axis everywhere behind the aperture (see Fig. 2). This result has been verified by experiment, and provides incontrovertible proof for the wave theory. The bright spot is sometimes called “Poisson’s spot” (after the particle theory advocate who said it should not exist) of the “spot of Arago” (after the experimentalist who verified its existence).
