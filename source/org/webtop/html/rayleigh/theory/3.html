Rayleigh Resolution Criterion
The Rayleigh Resolution Criterion

Let us now consider what the intensity on the observation screen will look like. If the angular separation q is large, then the Airy disks of the two sources do not overlap, and it is obvious that there are two point sources illuminating the lens. In this case we say that the two objects are resolved by the lens. If q is very small, the Airy disks of the two patterns can overlap so much that the intensity pattern on the screen can look like the pattern from a single point source. In this case we say that the two sources are not resolved.

Lord Rayleigh came up with a practical criterion (now referred to as the Rayleigh resolution criterion) for specifying under what conditions two sources are just resolved by a lens:

Two sources are said to be just resolved when the center of the diffraction pattern of one falls on the first minimum of the diffraction pattern of the other

For the Airy patterns from each source, the angular "distance" from the peak of the distribution to its first minimum is q1=1.22l/D. It therefore follows from the geometry (see Figure 4) that the center of one pattern of one falls on the first minimum of the other when the angular separation of the sources, q, is equal to q1. Thus the three possible situations can be classified by comparing the angular separation of the sources to q1:

(a) If q > q1, then the two sources are resolved. View Image
(b) If q = q1, then the two sources are just resolved. View Image
(c) If q < q1, then the two sources are not resolved. View Image

Examples of the three cases are depicted in Figures 5 through 7. (In each case Ic is the intensity at the center of the Airy pattern due to one source.)  In Figure 5, the angular separation of the objects is twice the Rayleigh angle, q=2qr. The top part of the figure shows plots of the intensities on the observation screen due to each source, for observation points along the x-axis,  I1(x,0) and I2(x,0). The bottom part of this figure shows the corresponding WebTOP visualization. The two objects are clearly resolved in this case.
