Diffraction - Rayleigh Resolution Criterion: Exercises

Rayleigh Resolution Criterion: When light from two monochromatic point sources is incident upon a lens, the two images formed are resolved if and only if their angular separation, q, obeys the condition q  < qmin , where qmin=1.22 l/D . Here l is the wavelength of the light, and D is the diameter of the lens. When q = qmin we say that the two images are barely resolved.

Exercise 1: Changing the Lens Diameter: Effect on the Individual Intensity Patterns
(a) Set Wavelength = 550 nm, Diameter = 3.355 mm, and Angle = 8.0E-5 rad. Use the Zoom icon to zoom out until the ends of the two gray poles are visible. Use the red cone widget on the rim of the lens to increase its diameter to approximately 5.0 mm. How did the intensity pattern on the observation screen change when you did this? Why did this happen?

(b) Repeat the steps described in (a) above, but this time focus your attention on the white intensity graph just above the observation screen. Describe what happens to this graph. Why did this happen?

Exercise 2: Changing the Wavelength of the Light: the Effect on Individual Intensity Patterns
Set Wavelength = 550 nm, Diameter = 3.355 mm, and Angle = 8.0E-5. Use the Wavelength keyboard entry box to change the wavelength to 400 nm. How did the intensity pattern on the observation screen change when you increased the wavelength of the light? Why did this happen?

Exercise 3: Changing the Focal Length of the Lens: Effect on Individual Intensity Patterns
Enter the following parameters into the data entry boxes at the botton of the WebTOP page: Wavelength = 400 nm and Diameter = 4.88 mm. In this module when you move the observation screen you also change the focal length of the lens (the observation screen is always in the focal plane of the lens). Use the red cone on the top of the observation screen to move the screen all the way to the back of the box it is inside of. Now move the screen towards the lens, stopping momentarily at at least two points along the way.

(a) Does the angular separation of the images of the two sources change?

(b) Does the spatial separation of the two images on the observation screen change?

(c) Describe how the intensity distribution of each image changes. (You will want to zoom out to see this.)

Exercise 4: The Resolution of Two Distant Sources: Changing Angular Separation of the Sources
(a) Set Wavelength = 550 nm, Diameter = 3.355 mm, and Angle = 4.0E-5 rad. This angle is twice as large as qmin for this situation. Are the two images resolved?

(b) While looking only at the intensity pattern on the observation screen (i.e., without looking at the parameter values at the bottom of the page), use the gray pole widget to decrease the angular separation of the two sources. Stop when you think the two images are barely resolved. Compare your result (see the Angle box on the console) to Rayleigh's qmin (see the Min Angle box on the console).

(c) Decrease the angular separation further, until it is approximately one-half of qmin. Are the images still resolved?
