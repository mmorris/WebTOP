2. Linear Polarizer

An ideal linear polarizer is a device that turns any type light into linearly polarized light. In this section we will discuss polarization by absorption and the effect of a linear polarizer on various types of light.

2.1 Basic Physics

We will limit our discussion to the most widely used linear polarizer, the Polaroid H-sheet.

An H-sheet is made in the following way. A sheet of clear polyvinyl alcohol is heated and stretched in a given direction, and its long hydrocarbon molecules become aligned in the process.

The sheet is then dipped into an ink solution rich in iodine. The iodine impregnates the plastic and attaches to the straight long-chain polymeric molecules, forming a chain of its own. The conduction electrons associated with the iodine are free to move along the chains, but not in the direction perpendicular to the chains.

Figure 17 is a “schematic” representation of an H-sheet. In this figure, the free electrons in each of the long molecules can move easily in the horizontal direction, but not in the vertical direction. When an incident electric field strikes the sheet, the component of the field parallel to the chains (the red component in the figure) oscillates the free electrons and does work on them, and is therefore strongly absorbed. The component perpendicular to the chains (the blue component in the figure) is not absorbed, it transmits through the sheet. As a result, the direction perpendicular to the chains is called the transmission axis (TA).
