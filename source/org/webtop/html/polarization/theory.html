Polarization: Theory

Introduction

In this module the propagation of a monochromatic, completely polarized, plane wave of light, or of an unpolarized plane wave of light is simulated. Specifically, the appropriate electric field vectors are displayed at a set of observation points in the direction of propagation (along the z-axis in Fig. 1 below), and are made to vary in time in the appropriate way. In addition, the user may place one or more filters (linear polarizers and/or wave plates) into the path of the propagating beam in order to see the effects they have on the polarization of the light.

Figure 1 The Polarization module. Circularly polarized traveling in the positive z-direction.

Unpolarized Electric Field

When the Unpolarized tab is selected WebTOP generates an electric field propagating in the positive z-direction that is, to a good approximation, unpolarized, i.e., the direction of the electric field varies randomly and rapidly in time. Linear Polarizer

Suppose an electric field, Ei(z,t), is propagating in the positive z-direction and is incident upon an ideal linear polarizer that is perpendicular to the z-axis and whose position on this axis is specified by the variable z. When light is incident upon such a polarizer, the component of the electric field vector that is parallel to the transmission axis (TA) of the polarizer passes through the polarizer undiminished in amplitude, and the component perpendicular to the TA is completely absorbed. There are two parameters that characterize such a polarizer: z, the position of the polarizer along the z-axis, and f, the angle that the polarizer's TA makes with the positive x-direction. In this module these parameters take on the values 0 < z < 20 and 0 < f < 360 degrees.

Wave Plates

A wave plate is a sheet of birefringent material, i.e., it has different indices of refraction in two orthogonal directions in the plane of its faces (the x- and y-directions in Fig. 6 below). This causes the optical path lengths travelled by the x- and y-components of the electric field to be different which, in general, causes a change in the polarization of the light.

Figure 6 Linearly polarized light incident upon a half-wave plate.

There are three parameters that characterize a wave plate in this module: z, the position of the wave plate along the z-axis, f, the angle that the wave plate's fast axis makes with the positive x-direction, and N, the number of waves of optical path length difference (between the two electric field components) that the plate provides. In this module these parameters take on the values 0 < z < 20 and 0 < f < 360 degrees, and 0 < N < 1. For a half-wave plate N = 0.50, and for a quarter-wave plate N =0.25.

For an example in which linearly polarized light is incident upon a half-wave plate, see Example 11. For an example in which linearly polarized light is incident upon a quarter-wave plate, see Example 12.
