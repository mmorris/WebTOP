Waves: Examples

Example 1 Waves From a Point Source - Properties

This example simulates the transverse, two-dimensional, circular waves generated when a point source performs simple harmonic motion on the surface of a membrane. The following source parameters vary, consecutively, during the simulation:
(a) the amplitude of its motion,
(b) the wavelength of the wave it generates (which depends on the frequency of its motion),
(c) the initial phase of its motion,
(d) its position in the x-y plane (the surface of the membrane).

Example 2 Two Point Sources – Effect of Changing the Distance Between the Sources

This example simulates the waves generated by two identical point sources that have the same amplitude, the same frequency (wavelength = 8), and the same initial phase. During the simulation the positions of the sources are varied, in order to vary the distance between the sources. The following pairs of points are used, consecutively, for the locations of the sources:
(a) (-50,-8) and (-50,8).  In this case the distance between the sources is equal to two wavelengths.
(b) (-50,-12) and (-50,12).  In this case distance between the sources is equal to three wavelengths.
(c) (-50,-16) and (-50,16). In this case the distance between the sources is equal to four wavelengths.

Example 3  Three Point Sources

This example simulates the waves generated by three identical point sources that have the same amplitude, the same frequency, and the same initial phase. The distance between each consecutive source is two wavelengths.

Example 4  Waves From a Line Source - Properties

This example simulates the waves generated by a line source that is touching the membrane and performing simple harmonic motion. The following line source parameters vary consecutively during the simulation:
(a) the amplitude of its motion,
(b) the wavelength of the wave it generates (which depends upon the frequency of its motion),
(c) the initial phase of its motion,
(d) the direction traveled by the wave it generates (specifically, the angle between the direction of propagation of the wave and the positive x-axis).

Example 5  Standing Waves

In this example the disturbance on the surface of the membrane is the superposition of two identical “linear” waves traveling in opposite directions. Specifically, the total disturbance on the membrane, call it y(x, y, t), can be written as

y(x,y,t) = y1(x,y,t) + y2(x,y,t), (1)

where y1(x, y, t) is a linear wave traveling in the positive x-direction, and y2(x, y, t) is a linear wave traveling in the negative x-direction,

y1(x,y,t) = A cos(kx – wt),   (2)
   
y2(x,y,t) = A cos(kx + wt), (3)

Here, A is the amplitude of each wave, k = 2p/l is its angular wave number, and w= kv is its angular frequency. Here l is the wavelength, and v is the wave speed.

Example 6  Superposition of Two Linear Waves Traveling in Different Directions

In this example the disturbance on the surface of the membrane is the superposition of two identical linear waves traveling in different directions. Let the waves have the same amplitudes, wavelengths, and initial phases. Let q1 be the angle of propagation for wave 1, and q2 be the angle of propagation for wave 2.  The wave functions for these waves, call them y1(x, y, t) and y2(x, y, t), are given by the formulas

y1(x,y,t) = A cos(kx cosq1 + kx sinq1 – wt),  (4)
   
y2(x,y,t) = A cos(kx cosq2 + kx sinq2 – wt), (5)

This example simulates the case q1 = 45 degrees and q2 = 135 degrees.

Example 7   Beats

In this example the disturbance on the surface of the water is the superposition of two line waves traveling of slightly different wavelengths (frequencies) . Let the waves have the same amplitude, A, same initial phase (zero), and same direction of propagation, q. Let l1 be the wavelength of wave 1, and l2 be the wavelength of wave 2.  The wave functions for these waves, call them y1(x, y, t) and y2(x, y, t), are given by the formulas

y1(x,y,t) = A cos(k1x  – w1 t),  (6)
   
y2(x,y,t) = A cos(k2 x – w2 t),  (7)

In this example the case l1 = 6.00  and l2 = 6.75 is simulated.
