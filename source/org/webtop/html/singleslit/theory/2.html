Eqs. (2.3) – (2.5) tell us something very important. Other than the scaling factor x/a, the shape of the intensity pattern does not depend upon a, l , and z separately, it depends on the Fresnel Number, Nx = a2/l z.

The Fresnel number can be therefore used to classify the physics that will occur in various regions of space after the aperture.

(a) If Nx >>1, we are in the Fresnel region.
Here the light is traveling mostly in the forward direction (i.e., in the positive z-direction), and there are a series maxima and minima in the intensity in the x-direction. It turns out that the number of major maxima which occur across the diffraction pattern (i.e., in the x-direction) is approximately equal to the Fresnel number.
(b) If Nx <<1, we are in the Fraunhofer region.
Here there is a large center lobe which increases in size linearly as you get further from the aperture, with side lobes that have much less energy than the center lobe.
(c) If Nx @ 1, this is the region where the transition from Fresnel diffraction to Fraunhofer diffraction occurs.
