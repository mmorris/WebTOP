Notation

The plane of incidence is the plane that contains the incident ray and the normal to the interface of the two media. For s-polarized light, the direction of each electric field vector is perpendicular to the plane of incidence. For p-polarized light, the direction of each electric field vector is parallel to the plane of incidence.

Example 1 s-Polarized Light Incident from Air onto Glass

In this example a monochromatic plane wave of s-polarized light is incident from air (nA = 1.00) onto a glass (nG = 1.50) slab.  The incident light is linearly polarized perpendicular to the plane of incidence. The incident, reflected and refracted electric field vectors are depicted at a series of observation points along the incident, reflected, and refracted rays. During the simulation three different angles of incidence are investigated:
(a) 30 degrees,
(b) 60 degrees, 
(c) 89.99 degrees. Note that in each case the incident electric field vector undergoes a p phase change upon reflection.

Example 2 p-Polarized Light Incident from Air onto Glass

In this example a monochromatic plane wave of light is incident from air (nA = 1.00) onto a glass (nG = 1.50) slab.  The light is linearly polarized parallel to the plane of incidence. The incident, reflected and refracted electric fields are depicted at a series of observation points along the corresponding rays, for three different angles of incidence:
(a) 30 degrees,
(b) 56.3 degrees, 
(c) 89.99 degrees. 

In case (a) there is no phase change upon reflection. In case (b) there is no reflected electric field. In case (c) the incident electric field vector undergoes a p phase change upon reflection.

Example 3 Polarization By Reflection: Air-to-Glass

In this example an unpolarized plane wave of light is incident from air (nA = 1.00) onto a glass (nG = 1.50) slab at Brewster’s angle, which is  56.3 degrees for the current situation. The incident, reflected and refracted electric fields are depicted at a series of observation points along the corresponding rays. Note that the reflected field is linearly polarized perpendicular to the plane of incidence.

Example 4 Polarization By Reflection: Glass-to-Air

In this example an unpolarized plane wave of light is incident from a glass (nG = 1.50) slab onto air at Brewster’s angle, which is 33.7 degrees for the current situation. The incident, reflected and refracted electric fields are depicted at a series of observation points along the corresponding rays. Note that the reflected field is linearly polarized perpendicular to the plane of incidence.

Example 5 s-Polarized Light Incident from Glass onto Air

In this example a monochromatic plane wave of light is incident from a glass (nG = 1.50) slab onto air.  The light is linearly polarized perpendicular to the plane of incidence. The incident, reflected and refracted electric fields are depicted at a series of observation points along the corresponding rays for four different angles of incidence:
(a) 30 degrees,
(b) 41 degrees,
(c) 42 degrees, 
(d) 89.99 degrees. 

In cases (a) and (b) there is no phase change upon reflection. In cases (c) and (d) the angle of incidence exceeds the critical angle, so total internal reflection occurs. The simulation does not depict the evanescent electric fields in the air.

Example 6 p-Polarized Light Incident from Glass onto Air

In this example a monochromatic plane wave of light is incident from a glass (nG = 1.50) slab onto air.  The light is linearly polarized parallel to the plane of incidence. The incident, reflected, and refracted electric fields are depicted at a series of observation points along the corresponding rays for four different angles of incidence:
(a) 30 degrees,
(b) 33.7 degrees,
(c) 41 degrees,
(d) 42 degrees, 
(e) 89.99 degrees. 

In cases (a) and (b) there is a phase change upon reflection. In case (c) there is no reflected electric field. In case (d) there is no phase change due to reflection. In case (e) there is a p phase change upon reflection.
