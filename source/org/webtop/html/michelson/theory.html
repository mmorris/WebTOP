Basic Ideas

Consider the schematic of the interferometer given in Fig. 2.
Figure 2 Schematic of a Michelson interferometer. S is a monochromatic point source. BS is a beam splitter, C is a compensator plate, M1 is the translation mirror, M2 is the tilt mirror, and S is an observation screen. SB1 is defined in the text below.

The interferometer works in the following way. The light from the point source S first strikes the beam splitter, which divides the beam into two equal amplitude beams: a reflected one, which we will call Beam 1, and a transmitted one, which we will call Beam 2. We assume that the back surface of the beam splitter is the coated surface.

Beam 1 travels to the "translation" mirror M1 of the interferometer. This mirror can translate back and forth in order to provide a desired path length difference d between the paths traveled by Beams 1 and 2.  Beam 1 then reflects off of the translation mirror and travels back to the beam splitter.  At the beam splitter it splits into two equal amplitude beams.  The beam of interest is the one that transmits through the beam splitter and travels to the observation screen. In Fig. 2, SB1 is the virtual image of S produced by Beam 1.

Let us now discuss Beam 2.  Beam 2 is the transmitted beam created when the light from the point source is incident upon the beam splitter.  It travels from the beam splitter through the compensator plate to the "tilt" mirror M2.  In this interferometer the tilt mirror can only be rotated about its vertical axis; we will call this angle of rotation f (see Fig 2).  (In most Michelson interferometers M2 can be tilted with respect to two orthogonal axes.)   The beam then reflects from the tilt mirror and travels back through the compensator plate to the beam splitter.  At the beam splitter, beam 2 is divided into equal amplitude reflected and transmitter beams.  We are interested in the reflected beam that travels to the observation screen. There, it interferes with the beam that has traveled in the other arm of the interferometer.  This module depicts the resulting intensity pattern on the observation screen, and displays a plot of the intensity as a function of position across the center of the pattern.

Intensity on the Observation Screen

(a) Mirror M2 not  Tilted (f= 0)

Let P be a point on the observation screen, and let q be its angular position (see Fig.2).  The intensity at P is given by the following formula

I(P)=4I1(P) sin2(kd cosq)
(1)

Here k=2p/l is the angular wave number of the light and I1(P) is the irradiance that would be observed at P if only one of the two beams were incident upon the observation screen.
