Diffraction - Transmission Grating: Examples

To view an example, press the button at the end of the description. Once the module finishes loading, press the play button at the recording panel. If you want to save the example code to your computer and run it locally right click of the disk icon and save the file to disk. Use wsl as an extension of the file you save to your computer.

Example 1 Two Spectral Lines Incident, Varying the Distance Between the Grooves

In this example light containing spectral lines of wavelengths 450 and 500 nm is normally incident upon a grating. Initially the spacing between the grooves is 1.5 microns. In this case we see that three diffracted orders for each wavelength occur on either side of the central order. The distance between the grooves is then changed to 2.00 microns. In this case we see that four diffracted orders for each wavelength occur on either side of the central order.

 

Example 2  Two Spectral Lines Incident, Varying the Number of Grooves

In this example light containing spectral lines of wavelengths 400 and 425 nm is normally incident upon a grating whose grooves are 2.00 microns apart. In this example our attention is focused on the two second-order spectral lines produced by the grating. Initially the number of grooves is 50. This is changed to 100, then back to 50, and then back to 100. Note that when the larger number of grooves is used, each diffracted line is narrower.

Example 3 Two Spectral Lines Incident, Varying the Distance Between the Grooves

In this example light containing spectral lines of wavelengths 450 and 500 nm is normally incident upon a grating whose grooves are 2.00 microns apart. Initially the groove width is 0.100 microns. The groove width is then increased to 0.200, 0.300, and 0.400 microns. It is then decreased to 0.300, 0.200, and 0.100 microns. Note that changing the groove width changes the relative intensities of different orders.

 

Exercise 4  The Visible Balmer Lines in Hydrogen

In this example light from a hydrogen spectral lamp is incident upon a grating whose grooves are 2.00 microns apart. The wavelengths for the H alpha, H beta, H gamma, and H delta lines, are, respectively, 410.2, 434.1, 486.1, and 656.3 nm. The lines are not equally "strong", i.e., equally bright. The brightness increases with wavelength. WebTOP takes this into consideration. In order to be able to display lines strengths are significantly different, the user can use the Intensity Factor Increase/Decrease buttons in the middle of the Console to raise or lower the level of all the lines. In this example, the first order diffracted spectra of hydrogen are observed. Initially only the two strongest first order lines (the H gamma and H delta lines) are clearly observable on the observation screen. The Intensity Factor Increase button is then used to increase the brightness of all four lines. The first order H alpha and H beta, lines now become visible. Note that when this is done the H gamma and H delta lines are "overexposed" (and become white.)
